Old People and Exercise

Many old people do not get much exercise. They sit and read, or watch TV all day, or sleep a lot. That is bad for them because everyone needs exercise to stay well. Exercise is important to keep strong and healthy. It's also fun to do exercise with other people. They can meet new friends and feel good, too.

Most children are active. They run and play with their friends, and they don't need to think about exercise. But old people usually need to think about when and where to exercise. Doctors say old people need some exercise every day. They can walk, swim, or work in a garden. It might also help to stop watching TV and using the computer so much. Exercise is always good, and any time of life is a good time to start.

Between Summer and Winter

Summer is the hottest season. Winter is the coldest. Between them is autumn. In many countries, autumn is a time of change. The air becomes wetter and cooler. Many plants and trees change. Their green leaves turn from green to colorful yellows, browns, and oranges. Then they die and fall to the ground. This is why autumn is called "fall" in many places.

When the leaves die, the trees stay alive. In fact, many trees need to lose their leaves. In summer, the tree drinks water from the earth. Then a lot of the water comes out of the leaves. In autumn, the tree closes the holes where the leaves were. So the tree keeps its water in winter. Then the tree grows new leaves every spring.

Punctuation and Capitalization Rules

Punctuation
Punctuation Rules and Examples
Period (.)

Use at the end of a statement and a command.

Music can help you learn a language.

Turn left at the light.

Appears at the end of many abbreviations.

Mrs., Mr., Dr.

a.m., p.m., etc.

St., Ave., U.S.A.
Comma (,)

Separates items in a series, or list, of three or more things.

The teacher needs pens, a tablet, paper, and an eraser.

Sets off place names (city, state or city, country), dates, and addresses.

I live in Tokyo, Japan.

My birthday is August 29, 2001.

He lives at 403 Branden Way, San Francisco, California.

Appears after the greeting and the closing of a letter.

Dear Sara,

Best regards,

Separates two independent clauses in a compound sentence.

I wanted to go cycling, but it was raining outside.

Brad worked on his English homework, and I washed the dishes.

Use a comma between two or more coordinate adjectives.

It was a cold, windy day.

He drove along the wet, slippery, and dangerous road.

Separates a dependent clause and an independent clause.

While the children were sleeping, Dad cleaned the house.

Separates introductory words, phrases, or clauses that come before a main clause.

Yes, I like to eat apples.

After school, he plays soccer in the park.

Sets off a descriptive word or phrase from the rest of the sentence.

Vijay and Karen, my neighbors, work at the hospital.

Use in writing a dialogue, or conversation. It appears between the words quoted and the rest of the sentence.

"Come back!" Kim said, "I see it now."

Use to tag a question in a sentence.

Mrs. Lee likes perfume, doesn't she?
Semicolon (;)

Joins two independent clauses.

It is raining today; Lin will not ride his bike to school.

Separates a list that includes commas.

His band will play in Brussels, Belgium; Mumbai, India; and Athens, Greece.
Colon (:)

Use after a complete statement to introduce related ideas or a list.

Summer is too hot: the crops have withered in the heat.

There are four seasons: spring, summer, winter, and fall.

Separates the hours and the minutes in time.

8:15 a.m.

Use after a greeting in a business letter.

Dear Sir or Madam:

Use before a list of items, especially after the words such as these and the following.

When you prepare for class, you should have the following: a tablet, a dictionary, and writing supplies.
Exclamation Point (!)

Shows strong emotion with a single word or in a sentence.

Wow!

I can't believe it!
Question Mark (?)

Ends a sentence that is a question.

You are coming with us, aren't you?

Who is the next person in line?
Quotation Marks (" ")

Show the exact words a person says.

"I can't do this," Mary said.

Kaylin said, "I love ice cream!"

Set off the title of a short poem, a song, or an article.

Joy read the poem, "Fog," aloud.

Set off a word or phrase that is used as a technical term or in an unusual way.

This literary technique is known as "flashback."

The characters "live" in a futuristic world.
Apostrophe (')

Shows missing letters in a contraction.

Ling didn't know the answer.

Shows possession or ownership.

That is my sister's video game.

The students' backpacks were on the back table.
Parentheses ( )

Show additional, nonessential information in a sentence.

Frank's tennis partner (his younger sister) plays like a champion.
Capitalization
Rules and Examples

Begin the first word in a sentence with a capital letter.

He rides the train to school.

Capitalize the pronoun I.

After school, I do my chores and then study.

Capitalize the first word and the important words in the titles of books, magazines, newspapers, and songs.

The New York Times

The Grapes of Wrath

Capitalize place names, including: cities, countries, planets, mountains, oceans, rivers, monuments, schools, streets, etc.

We visited the Eiffel Tower in Paris, France.

Lincoln High School is on Boundary Road.

Capitalize the names of people.

My teacher is Mr. Pasko.

Mom and Dad are helping Uncle Tom.

Capitalize the days of the week and the names of the months.

My birthday is on Wednesday this year.

Alicia's favorite month is July.

Capitalize the names of races, languages, religions, holidays, and special events.

Marco speaks French.

Let's dress up for Halloween.

Did you watch the Olympics?

Capitalize the first word in a dialogue to signal a person's exact words.

Li asked, "Where is my jacket?"

Capitalize the names of organizations, the names of companies, and product trade names.

My favorite shoes are made by Nike.

Capitalize the names of historical events and time periods.

The Black Death killed millions of people in the Middle Ages.

Capitalize the initials of acronyms.

The U.S. and the U.K. are two founding members of NATO.
